Common use

Kisqali is a targeted therapy used to treat hormone receptor-positive (HR+), HER2-negative (HER2-) breast cancer, specifically for advanced or metastatic cases or early-stage (II or III) high-risk cases. It is a CDK4/6 inhibitor that blocks proteins encouraging cancer growth.

Dosage and direction

Do not change your dose or stop taking Kisqali without talking to your healthcare provider. Kisqali is usually taken 1 time a day for 21 days, followed by 7 days off treatment. This is called a cycle of treatment. Kisqali may be taken with or without food. Swallow Kisqali tablets whole. Do not chew, crush, or split Kisqali tablets before swallowing them. Take Kisqali each day at about the same time, preferably in the morning. Do not take any Kisqali tablets that are broken, cracked, or that look damaged.

Precautions

Before taking Kisqali, tell your healthcare provider about all of your medical conditions, including if you have: Any heart problems, including heart failure, irregular heartbeats, and QT prolongation; Ever had a heart attack; A slow heartbeat (bradycardia); High blood pressure that is not controlled; Decreased thyroid gland function (hypothyroidism); Problems with the amount of potassium, calcium, phosphorus, or magnesium in your blood; A fever, chills, or any other signs or symptoms of infection; Liver problems; Kidney problems; Plans to become pregnant, or are pregnant; Plans to breastfeed or are breastfeeding.

Contraindications

Kisqali is contraindicated in patients with a known severe hypersensitivity (allergy) to ribociclib, peanut-related allergies (due to soya lecithin in some formulations), or severe underlying cardiac issues, significant arrhythmia. It is generally not recommended in moderate to severe liver impairment without dosage adjustments.
Kisqali can harm your unborn baby. If you can become pregnant, your healthcare provider should do a pregnancy test before you start treatment with Kisqali. Talk to your healthcare provider about birth control methods that may be right for you during this time. It is not known if Kisqali passes into your breast milk. Do not breastfeed during treatment with Kisqali and for at least 3 weeks after the last dose of Kisqali.

Possible side effect

The most common side effects of Kisqali: Decreased white blood cell counts; Decreased red blood cell counts; Increased liver function tests; Infections; Increased kidney function tests; Decreased platelet counts; Nausea; Headache; Tiredness.

Drug interaction

Tell your healthcare provider about all the medicines you take, including prescription and over-the-counter medicines, vitamins, and herbal supplements. Especially tell your healthcare provider if you take: CYP3A inhibitors: such as clarithromycin, erythromycin, diltiazem, itraconazole, ketoconazole, ritonavir, verapamil, goldenseal, and grapefruit; CYP3A4 inducers, such as phenobarbital, phenytoin, rifampicin, St. John's Wort, and glucocorticoids; CYP3A substrates, such as midazolam, felodipine, buspirone, lovastatin, eletriptan, sildenafil, simvastatin, triazolam, dexamethasone, antipyrine, quinine; QT-prolonging drugs, such as haloperidol, ziprasidone, quetiapine, thioridazine, olanzapine, risperidone, droperidol, amiodarone, sotalol, dofetilide, procainamide, quinidine, flecainide, macrolides, fluoroquinolones.

Missed dose

If you miss a dose of Kisqali or vomit after taking a dose of Kisqali, do not take another dose on that day. Take your next dose at your regular time.

Overdose

Potential symptoms of an overdose include severe nausea, vomiting, haematological toxicity and heart rhythm issue. If you suspect an overdose, contact a doctor or nearest emergency department immediately.

Storage

Store at room temperature between 68°F and 77°F (20°C and 25°C). Store in the original blister package to protect from moisture. Keep out of the reach of children and pets.
